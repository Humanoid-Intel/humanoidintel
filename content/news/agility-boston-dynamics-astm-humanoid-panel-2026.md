# What Can Humanoids Actually Do in Warehouses Today?

Agility Robotics, Boston Dynamics, and ASTM International are convening a panel to address the gap between humanoid robotics hype and industrial reality. The discussion comes as Agility's Digit robots operate in fewer than 10 commercial facilities globally, while Boston Dynamics' Atlas remains primarily research-focused despite recent electric actuator upgrades.

The panel represents a critical inflection point for the $7.3 billion humanoid robotics market. While venture funding reached record highs in 2025, actual deployments remain limited to highly controlled environments. Agility's Digit, the most commercially deployed humanoid with its 20+ DOF configuration, currently handles simple pick-and-place tasks at Amazon and GXO facilities. Boston Dynamics' hydraulic-to-electric Atlas transition signals serious commercialization intent, but the company has yet to announce concrete deployment timelines.

The ASTM involvement is particularly significant—the standards organization's F45 Committee on Robotics has been developing safety protocols for collaborative humanoids since 2023. Their participation suggests the industry is moving beyond proof-of-concept toward standardized deployment frameworks. However, current humanoids still struggle with dexterous manipulation tasks that require sub-millimeter precision, limiting their warehouse applications to basic material handling rather than complex assembly operations.

## Current Deployment Reality Check

Agility's Digit represents the most advanced commercial humanoid deployment today, but the numbers reveal modest scope. The company has delivered approximately 50 units across pilot programs, with monthly lease rates reportedly between $10,000-15,000. Each Digit can handle payloads up to 16 kg and operates for 4-6 hours on a single charge—sufficient for specific warehouse tasks but far from the 24/7 operations many logistics companies require.

Boston Dynamics' Atlas evolution from hydraulic to electric actuation addresses key commercial barriers. The new electric version eliminates the 5kW hydraulic power unit requirement and reduces maintenance complexity. However, the company hasn't disclosed pricing or availability timelines. Industry sources suggest Atlas units could command $200,000+ per robot, positioning them as premium automation solutions rather than broad warehouse deployment options.

The panel timing aligns with growing skepticism about humanoid ROI. McKinsey's 2025 robotics survey found that 73% of logistics executives view current humanoid solutions as "overhyped for near-term deployment." The primary technical barriers remain: limited battery life, high per-unit costs, and insufficient dexterous manipulation capabilities for complex tasks.

## Standards Development and Safety Protocols

ASTM's F45.05 subcommittee has been developing humanoid-specific safety standards since late 2023, focusing on collaborative workspace protocols. The draft standards address critical deployment concerns: fail-safe mechanisms when humanoids operate alongside human workers, emergency stop procedures, and workspace boundary definitions.

These standards become crucial as humanoids move beyond research labs into production environments. Current OSHA guidelines don't specifically address bipedal robots, creating regulatory uncertainty for manufacturers. The ASTM standards development suggests industry recognition that deployment success requires regulatory clarity, not just technical advancement.

The panel will likely address sim-to-real transfer challenges that continue plaguing commercial deployments. While companies demonstrate impressive capabilities in controlled environments, real warehouse conditions—varying lighting, floor surfaces, and object placement—still challenge current perception systems and whole-body control algorithms.

## Market Implications and Trajectory

This panel discussion signals a maturation phase for humanoid robotics, moving from venture capital excitement toward practical deployment realities. The involvement of standards organizations suggests the industry is preparing for scaled deployments, but current technical limitations suggest widespread adoption remains 2-3 years away.

The warehouse automation market represents a $6.8 billion opportunity for humanoid robotics, but current solutions compete against proven alternatives. Traditional AMRs and articulated arms often provide better ROI for specific tasks. Humanoids need to demonstrate clear advantages—form factor flexibility and human workspace compatibility—to justify their higher costs and complexity.
