Goldman Sachs' Equity Research division published a revised humanoid robotics sector note on January 3, 2026, lifting its total addressable market estimate to $74 billion by 2035 — nearly double the $38 billion figure from the same team's 2023 report and 48% above its intermediate $50 billion estimate from mid-2024. The revision is driven by three factors: faster-than-projected enterprise pilot conversions to production contracts, downward revision of expected hardware unit costs, and an upward revision to the addressable task set as AI capabilities have improved more rapidly than expected.

## What Changed in Goldman's Model

Goldman's original 2023 TAM model assumed a 2028 commercial inflection point, defined as the year humanoid deployments would exceed 10,000 cumulative units outside of one company's internal use. That milestone has already been reached — earlier than modeled — with Tesla's 8,000-unit internal deployment supplemented by a growing base of commercial units from Figure AI, Agility Robotics, and Apptronik.

The revised model makes four structural changes:

**1. Unit cost assumptions revised downward**

Goldman's 2023 model assumed first-generation commercial humanoids would price at $250,000-$400,000 per unit. The actual market has cleared significantly lower: Figure AI's BMW deployment is estimated by Goldman at $150,000-$180,000 per unit at current volumes, with a modeled learning curve to $40,000-$60,000 by 2030 based on manufacturing scale assumptions derived from EV cost curves. The lower price point expands the addressable market considerably, because at $250,000+ the ROI case requires very high-value tasks; at $60,000, the ROI case works for a much broader set of factory automation applications.

**2. Addressable task set expanded**

The 2023 model assumed humanoids would address approximately 4% of total manufacturing task-hours by 2035, limited to tasks meeting strict criteria: high repetition, moderate precision, bounded environment. Goldman's revised model increases this to 8-10% of manufacturing task-hours, acknowledging that AI capability improvements (specifically VLA models like Pi's π0.2) have expanded the set of tasks addressable without environment modification.

**3. Faster enterprise adoption curve**

Goldman's adoption S-curve, updated with actual pilot-to-contract conversion data, shows enterprise adoption tracking 18-24 months ahead of the 2023 projection. The BMW, Amazon, and Apptronik automotive contracts — none of which existed in 2023 — provide the first real data points for enterprise conversion rates. Goldman estimates conversion from pilot to multi-year contract at approximately 65%, above the 40% assumed in the 2023 model.

**4. Adjacent market expansion**

The 2023 TAM was largely confined to manufacturing and logistics. The 2026 revision adds three new verticals at meaningful scale:

- **Healthcare/eldercare**: $8.2B TAM contribution by 2035, driven by demographic aging in Japan, South Korea, Germany, and China, and regulatory changes enabling humanoid-assisted patient handling
- **Construction**: $4.5B contribution, driven by acute skilled trades labor shortages and the first credible demonstrations of humanoid framing and finishing tasks
- **Defense/government**: $3.1B contribution, driven by US DoD procurement interest in logistics and hazardous environment applications

Goldman's $74B 2035 estimate decomposes across verticals as follows:

| General manufacturing | $7.8B | Injection molding, metalworking support, packaging |

| Retail/hospitality | $2.6B | Stocking, cleaning, customer-facing assistance |

Automotive manufacturing remains the largest single vertical, consistent with the actual commercial deployment pattern. The electronics assembly estimate ($14.2B) is striking — it implies that humanoids will penetrate consumer electronics and semiconductor supply chain manufacturing, currently dominated by highly specialized fixed automation. Goldman's thesis is that the complexity and configuration frequency of electronics assembly makes fixed automation uneconomical for all but the highest-volume standardized components, while humanoids' flexibility becomes economically superior for the long tail.

## Bear vs. Bull Cases

Goldman presents explicit bear and bull scenarios bracketing the $74B base case:

**Bull Case ($138B by 2035)**

Assumes: Tesla releases Optimus for external sale at $25,000-$35,000 in 2026, catalyzing mass market adoption; Physical Intelligence or a comparable foundation model company achieves general manipulation capability enabling deployment without task-specific training; and Chinese humanoid exports expand despite current US tariff environment.

The bull case implies 5-7 million cumulative humanoid units by 2035 at an average price of approximately $25,000.

**Bear Case ($28B by 2035)**

Assumes: Reliability challenges at scale create high warranty and maintenance costs that erode fleet operator economics; regulatory barriers (OSHA, EU AI Act, workers' union resistance) slow enterprise adoption; and battery/energy density limitations prevent 8+ hour operating shifts required for factory economics.

The bear case implies continued niche deployment primarily at vertically integrated companies (Tesla, Amazon) that control their own deployment environments, with limited third-party commercial scale.

**Base Case ($74B by 2035)**

Assumes: Commercial humanoid unit prices decline from current ~$150,000 to approximately $45,000 by 2032 following semiconductor-like cost curves; manipulation AI achieves 85%+ task success on trained tasks in production environments; and enterprise procurement normalizes through established integration playbooks by 2028.

## What Goldman Gets Wrong

Goldman's model, like most sell-side TAM analysis, conflates capability with adoption. The $74B estimate assumes enterprise procurement organizations will evaluate, validate, integrate, and scale humanoid deployments at a pace determined primarily by unit economics. In practice, enterprise technology adoption — especially for physical automation that touches safety-critical operations — is constrained by procurement velocity, insurance and liability resolution, and workforce change management.

The sectors Goldman is most bullish on (electronics assembly, healthcare) are also the sectors with the most regulatory friction. Class II and III medical device environments have FDA implications for autonomous robotic systems. Electronics cleanrooms have contamination standards that humanoid robots have not yet been qualified against. These are solvable problems, but they add 2-4 years to any deployment timeline in those verticals.

Goldman's bull case, in particular, depends heavily on Tesla external sales at mass-market pricing. If that product decision is delayed 18 months, the bull case collapses to the base case — and the base case itself assumes a cost curve steeper than any humanoid company has publicly committed to.

## FAQ

**How does Goldman's $74B estimate compare to other analyst forecasts?**
Goldman's revised estimate is the most bullish among major bank research desks. Bank of America's latest estimate (December 2025) is $52B by 2035; Morgan Stanley projects $46B. The divergence is primarily in assumed unit costs by 2030: Goldman models $45,000, BofA models $65,000, and Morgan Stanley models $80,000. All three agree automotive and logistics are the primary near-term verticals.

**Does the $74B TAM include robot-as-a-service revenue?**
Goldman's estimates are expressed in total economic value, encompassing hardware sales, RaaS subscription revenue, and associated software and services. A hardware-only TAM would be approximately 40-50% of the total, with the remainder split between RaaS subscriptions and services/support revenue streams.

**Is the $74B TAM addressable by existing companies or does it assume new entrants?**
Goldman's model does not assume that current companies capture the entire TAM. The estimate represents total potential market spend, with the expectation that the 2035 market will include: current leaders scaled up (Figure AI, Tesla Optimus, Apptronik), Chinese players with US market access, and new entrants from industrial automation incumbents (ABB, FANUC, Kuka) that have not yet entered the humanoid market but have the distribution and integration relationships to compete if the technology matures.
