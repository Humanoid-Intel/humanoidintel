# How Did an Oregon State Professor Build a $2.5 Billion Humanoid Robot Company?

Oregon State University robotics professor Jonathan Hurst co-founded Agility Robotics, which has achieved a $2.5 billion valuation making it one of the most valuable humanoid robotics companies globally. Hurst, who remains on OSU's faculty while serving as Chief Technology Officer at Agility, has spent over two decades developing bipedal locomotion technology that powers the company's flagship Digit humanoid robot.

The $2.5 billion valuation positions Agility Robotics alongside Boston Dynamics (acquired by Hyundai for $1.1 billion) and Figure AI ($2.6 billion valuation) as the industry's most valuable pure-play humanoid robotics companies. Agility's breakthrough came through Hurst's research on passive dynamic walking and spring-loaded inverted pendulum models, which enabled energy-efficient bipedal locomotion without the energy-intensive control systems used by earlier humanoid designs.

Digit represents a third-generation evolution of Hurst's bipedal research, featuring 20 degrees of freedom, torque-dense electric actuators, and proprietary leg springs that reduce power consumption by approximately 40% compared to rigid-joint alternatives. The robot has demonstrated autonomous warehouse operations at Amazon fulfillment centers and manufacturing facilities at Ford.

## From Academic Research to Commercial Reality

Hurst's journey began at OSU's Dynamic Robotics Laboratory, where he focused on understanding natural locomotion principles. His early work on ATRIAS (Assume The Robot Is A Sphere) established fundamental insights into dynamic stability that would later inform Digit's control architecture.

The transition from academic research to commercial viability required solving the sim-to-real gap that plagued earlier bipedal robots. Hurst's team developed novel approaches to whole-body control that enabled robust performance across varied terrain and unexpected perturbations. This research culminated in Cassie, a bipedal research platform that demonstrated unprecedented agility including a 5K run and backflip capabilities.

"The key insight was treating the robot as a dynamic system rather than trying to control every joint position," Hurst explained in technical presentations. This philosophy enabled Agility to achieve backdrivable actuators and compliant control systems that dramatically improved energy efficiency and robustness.

## Digit's Commercial Deployment Strategy

Agility has secured pilot deployments with major logistics companies, leveraging Digit's 1.75-meter height and 40-kilogram payload capacity for warehouse automation. The robot's tendon-driven hands provide sufficient dexterity for tote manipulation while maintaining the robust locomotion capabilities essential for industrial environments.

Recent software updates have integrated foundation models for natural language task specification, enabling operators to command Digit using conversational interfaces. This represents a significant advancement over traditional programmatic control systems that required specialized robotics expertise.

The company raised $150 million in Series C funding in 2023, with participation from DCVC, Playground Global, and Amazon's Industrial Innovation Fund. This brings total funding to approximately $350 million across multiple rounds.

## Industry Trajectory and Competitive Positioning

Agility's $2.5 billion valuation reflects investor confidence in the humanoid robotics market, which Goldman Sachs projects will reach $38 billion by 2035. However, technical challenges remain significant, particularly in dexterous manipulation and long-duration autonomous operation.

Compared to Tesla's Optimus program, Agility has prioritized commercial deployment over mass production scalability. While Tesla targets eventual $20,000 unit costs through automotive manufacturing techniques, Agility focuses on premium applications where Digit's advanced locomotion capabilities justify higher pricing.

The competitive landscape includes Boston Dynamics' Atlas (research-focused), Honda's ASIMO (discontinued), and emerging challengers like 1X Technologies and Sanctuary AI. Agility's advantage lies in proven outdoor locomotion capabilities and existing commercial partnerships.
