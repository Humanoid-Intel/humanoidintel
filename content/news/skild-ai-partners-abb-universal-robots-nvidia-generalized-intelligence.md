# How is Skild AI expanding robot intelligence partnerships?

Skild AI has announced strategic partnerships with ABB Robotics, Universal Robots, and NVIDIA to deploy its generalized robot intelligence platform across industrial applications. The collaboration aims to integrate Skild's vision-language-action (VLA) models with existing robotic hardware platforms, potentially accelerating zero-shot generalization capabilities in manufacturing and warehouse environments.

Founded by former OpenAI and Google DeepMind researchers, Skild AI has raised $300 million in Series A funding to develop foundational AI models for robotics. The company's approach focuses on training large-scale models that can transfer learned behaviors across different robot morphologies without task-specific programming. This partnership strategy represents a shift toward embedding general-purpose intelligence into established industrial robot ecosystems rather than developing proprietary hardware platforms.

The timing aligns with growing enterprise demand for adaptable automation solutions that can handle variability in manufacturing processes. ABB's industrial robot portfolio includes over 5,000 different models, while Universal Robots dominates the collaborative robot market with approximately 50% market share globally.

## Partnership Structure and Technical Integration

The three-way partnership positions each company to leverage complementary strengths in the robotics intelligence stack. ABB Robotics brings industrial-grade hardware platforms and deep manufacturing expertise, while Universal Robots provides the cobot infrastructure widely deployed across small and medium enterprises.

NVIDIA's role centers on providing the computational backbone through its robotics simulation platform Isaac Sim and the recently announced GR00T foundation model architecture. The collaboration will likely utilize NVIDIA's Jetson Thor chips for edge inference, enabling real-time decision-making without cloud connectivity dependencies.

Skild's VLA models are designed to process multimodal inputs—combining visual perception, natural language instructions, and proprioceptive feedback—to generate appropriate motor commands. The challenge lies in adapting these models to the specific kinematics and control systems of ABB's IRB series and Universal Robots' UR series platforms, which operate on fundamentally different architectures.

## Market Implications for Industrial Robotics

This partnership validates the emerging consensus that general-purpose AI will penetrate industrial robotics through software-first approaches rather than ground-up hardware development. Unlike humanoid-focused companies such as Figure AI or 1X Technologies, which are building complete robotic systems, Skild is pursuing a platform strategy that could reach market faster through existing hardware channels.

The industrial robotics market, valued at approximately $45 billion globally, has traditionally relied on task-specific programming and extensive integration services. Skild's approach promises to reduce deployment complexity by enabling robots to learn new tasks through demonstration rather than explicit programming.

However, significant technical hurdles remain. Industrial environments demand reliability levels exceeding 99.9% uptime, and the sim-to-real gap for VLA models in unstructured manufacturing scenarios has not been conclusively demonstrated at scale. Safety certification for AI-driven industrial robots also presents regulatory challenges that could slow adoption.

## Competitive Landscape Analysis

The partnership positions Skild AI in direct competition with other robotics foundation model companies, including Physical Intelligence (Pi), Covariant, and Google DeepMind's RT-X program. Physical Intelligence recently raised $400 million to develop similar general-purpose robotics models, while Covariant has already deployed its AI platform across hundreds of warehouse automation systems.

Skild's advantage lies in its experienced founding team, led by former OpenAI researchers who contributed to GPT development, and its focus on large-scale model training. The company has reportedly trained models on over 1 billion robot interaction data points, though this figure includes simulated environments.

The key differentiator will be execution speed and the ability to demonstrate measurable improvements in task success rates compared to traditional programming approaches. Early enterprise pilots will likely focus on pick-and-place operations, assembly tasks, and quality inspection—areas where visual understanding and fine motor control converge.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What makes Skild AI's approach different from other robotics AI companies?**
Skild AI focuses on developing foundation models that work across different robot hardware platforms, rather than building proprietary robots. This software-first strategy allows faster market penetration through partnerships with established manufacturers like ABB and Universal Robots.

**How do vision-language-action models work in industrial robotics?**
VLA models process visual input from cameras, natural language instructions from operators, and proprioceptive feedback from robot sensors to generate appropriate motor commands. This enables robots to understand and execute complex tasks without explicit programming for each scenario.

**What are the main technical challenges for deploying AI in industrial robotics?**
Key challenges include bridging the sim-to-real gap, achieving the 99.9%+ reliability required in manufacturing, obtaining safety certifications for AI-driven systems, and adapting general models to specific robot kinematics and control systems.

**How does this partnership compare to humanoid robotics companies?**
While humanoid companies like Figure AI build complete robotic systems, Skild's partnership strategy leverages existing industrial robot infrastructure. This approach potentially reaches market faster but faces different technical constraints related to adapting AI to diverse hardware platforms.

**What types of tasks will these AI-powered robots handle first?**
Initial deployments will likely focus on pick-and-place operations, assembly tasks, and quality inspection—applications where visual understanding, dexterous manipulation, and adaptability provide clear value over traditional programmed automation.
