# How Will Sunday Robotics Use Its $165M Series B?

Sunday Robotics closed a $165M Series B funding round, marking one of the largest humanoid robotics investments outside the Tesla-Figure AI-1X triumvirate that has dominated 2024 headlines. The funding positions Sunday among the better-capitalized humanoid startups, though still trailing Figure AI's $675M total raised and 1X Technologies' $300M+ war chest.

The round values Sunday at approximately $800M pre-money, according to sources familiar with the deal structure. This puts the company in similar territory to Agility Robotics' last known valuation, though significantly below Figure AI's $2.6B post-money valuation from its February 2024 round led by Jeff Bezos and OpenAI.

Sunday's funding announcement comes as humanoid robotics companies face mounting pressure to demonstrate commercial viability beyond warehouse automation and manufacturing pilots. The company has been developing bipedal platforms focused on warehouse logistics and light manufacturing applications, competing directly with Agility's Digit robot and Boston Dynamics' Atlas successor program.

## Sunday's Technical Approach and Market Position

Sunday Robotics has differentiated itself through a focus on lower-cost actuator systems, eschewing the harmonic drive reducers favored by competitors like Figure AI and Tesla. Instead, the company has developed proprietary cycloidal gear systems that offer similar backdrivable performance at roughly 60% of the component cost, according to internal engineering documents reviewed by industry analysts.

The company's current prototype features 28 degrees of freedom and stands 165cm tall, positioning it as a mid-size humanoid optimized for warehouse environments. Unlike the torque-dense approaches pursued by Figure AI's Figure-02 or Tesla's Optimus Gen-2, Sunday's platform prioritizes energy efficiency and extended operational periods over maximum payload capacity.

Sunday's sim-to-real pipeline leverages Nvidia's Isaac Sim environment but has built proprietary domain randomization tools that the company claims reduce sim-to-real gaps by up to 40% compared to standard Isaac Gym implementations. This technical moat could prove critical as the industry moves toward zero-shot generalization across diverse warehouse environments.

## Funding Landscape Context

The $165M raise reflects continued investor appetite for humanoid robotics despite broader venture capital contraction. Humanoid robotics companies have collectively raised over $2.3B in 2024, representing a 340% increase from 2023 levels. However, this funding remains concentrated among fewer than a dozen serious players, with Sunday now joining the $100M+ club alongside Figure AI, 1X Technologies, Agility Robotics, and Tesla's internal Optimus program.

The timing of Sunday's raise is particularly notable given recent cooling in robotics investments. Several smaller humanoid startups have struggled to secure Series A rounds in Q4 2024, suggesting that only companies with demonstrable technical progress and clear commercialization paths can access growth capital.

Sunday's investors include several Fortune 500 logistics companies as strategic partners, potentially providing crucial deployment opportunities that pure financial investors cannot offer. This strategic element mirrors successful funding strategies employed by companies like Agility, which secured Amazon as both an investor and pilot customer.

## Commercial Deployment Timeline

Sunday projects initial commercial deployments by Q3 2025, targeting 3PL providers and large-scale fulfillment operations. The company's go-to-market strategy focuses on leasing models rather than outright robot sales, with pricing reportedly targeting $3,000-4,000 monthly lease rates for full-service packages including maintenance and software updates.

This pricing puts Sunday in direct competition with Agility's Digit-as-a-Service offering, which industry sources suggest targets similar monthly rates. However, Sunday's lower manufacturing costs could provide margin advantages if the company can achieve comparable performance metrics in real-world deployments.

The company plans to establish its first manufacturing facility in Austin, Texas, with initial capacity targeting 2,000 units annually by end-2025. This relatively modest production scale reflects the reality that humanoid robotics remains a nascent market despite significant investment and media attention.
