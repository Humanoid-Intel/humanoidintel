Good morning. This is the inaugural edition of the HumanoidIntel Weekly Brief — your Friday digest of the week's most consequential developments in humanoid robotics, written for operators, investors, and engineers who need signal, not noise.

## 1. Figure AI's $1.5B Series C Changes the Financing Landscape

Figure AI closed $1.5 billion at a $6.7 billion post-money valuation this week, and the strategic composition of the round matters as much as the size. Samsung Securities and LG Technology Ventures leading a US humanoid round is a first — Korean consumer electronics capital entering what has been a domain of US tech VCs and corporate strategics. Both investors have obvious deployment motivations: Samsung and LG compete with Foxconn and other Chinese contract manufacturers who face the same labor economics driving humanoid adoption.

BMW iVentures' participation as a strategic investor formalizes what was already the most consequential commercial humanoid deployment in the Western world. BMW's internal data on Figure 02 performance is proprietary, but the willingness to write a check alongside the financing tells you what the operational numbers show.

For the sector overall: Figure's $6.7B valuation sets a new ceiling for comparable company valuations. Apptronik, which raised at ~$1.5B in November, is already likely marked up in its investors' portfolios. The comps are moving fast. The open question is whether secondary market transactions start emerging — the first sign that the asset class is maturing.

The concurrent Figure 03 mass production announcement is as significant as the capital. Production humanoids being shipped to customers in Q1 2026 means that by Q3, there will be real-world data on Figure 03 reliability, task performance, and per-unit economics under sustained commercial operation. That data will be the most important input to the sector's 2026 narrative.

## 2. Goldman's $74B TAM: The Revision That Matters

Goldman Sachs lifted its 2035 humanoid TAM from $38B to $74B this month, and while sell-side TAM estimates should always be read with appropriate skepticism, this revision is worth studying carefully because of *what changed*.

The prior $38B estimate assumed a 2028 commercial inflection point. Goldman now believes that inflection is already underway — the 8,000-unit Tesla Optimus fleet, combined with Figure's BMW deployment and Apptronik's automotive contract, constitute the inflection, not its prerequisite. The pull-forward of the inflection date by 2 years compounds through the adoption S-curve, nearly doubling the 2035 estimate.

The revised model's most interesting addition is the electronics assembly vertical at $14.2B — larger than logistics ($12.6B). Goldman's thesis is that the frequency of SKU changes in consumer electronics makes fixed automation economically suboptimal for the long tail of products, while humanoid flexibility captures this value. This is the right analysis, but the timeline is aggressive: electronics cleanroom qualification for humanoids is a 3-5 year engineering challenge that Goldman's model appears to smooth over.

The bear case ($28B) is not implausible. It requires reliability failures at scale, regulatory friction, and absence of Tesla external sales — none of which can be ruled out. Sophisticated investors in this space should be underwriting the base case while scenario-planning the bear.

## 3. The Actuator Architecture War Is Heating Up

Our deep-dive this week on tendon-driven vs. harmonic drive actuators landed with significant reader response. The executive summary for those who missed it: harmonic drives dominate commercial humanoids today because they're reliable and precise; tendon-driven systems offer a higher dexterity ceiling but have unsolved reliability problems.

What we didn't fully address in the piece: the trajectory. The current generation of commercial humanoids (Figure 03, Apollo, Digit v4) are all using harmonic drives as their primary transmission, optimizing for the deployment use cases that pay the bills today — structured assembly, logistics, automotive. But every company building toward the next platform generation is exploring backdrivable, compliant alternatives for manipulation joints.

Apptronik's Series Elastic Actuator heritage gives it the most mature force-controlled manipulation capability of any commercial platform. If the automotive contract proves that force-controlled assembly has enterprise demand, expect other platforms to accelerate their compliance architecture roadmaps.

Clone Robotics P1 remains the fascinating outlier: a fully tendon-driven, 206-DOF humanoid pursuing an architectural strategy that everyone acknowledges as theoretically superior and practically unproven. If the Myofiber technology achieves the reliability threshold for sustained operation, it represents the most significant hardware discontinuity in the sector. Watch for P1 longevity data in 2026 — the company's early-access home trial program will generate the first extended operating data on the platform.
